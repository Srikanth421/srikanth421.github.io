Introduction to NLP

⚙ Technical

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a branch of AI that enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language.

The pipeline flows: Processing → Understanding → Generation

Applications:

💬 Chatbots 🌐 Translation ❤ Sentiment Analysis 📄 Summarization 🎙 Speech Recognition 📧 Email Filtering 🔍 Grammar Checking
💡 Analogy

Teaching an Alien Human Language

Imagine an alien just landed on Earth. It sees humans making sounds and typing symbols — but has no idea what any of it means.

NLP is the science of teaching that alien to understand language — not just memorize words, but grasp context, tone, sarcasm, and meaning.

Without NLP, a computer is just that alien staring blankly at a book.

One-Hot Encoding

⚙ Technical

Each word gets a vector with a single 1 and all other values as 0. Words have no relationship to each other.

Cat
1000
Dog
0100
Tiger
0010
Plane
0001
⚠ Distance(Cat, Dog) = Distance(Cat, Plane) — the model thinks they're equally unrelated!
💡 Analogy

Random Locker Numbers

Imagine assigning locker numbers randomly to students. Locker 101 = Cat, Locker 247 = Dog, Locker 388 = Tiger.

Just because Cat's locker (101) is numerically closer to Dog's locker (247) doesn't tell you Cat and Dog are related. The numbers are completely arbitrary.

One-hot encoding does the same — it gives words ID numbers but captures no semantic meaning.

💡 Analogy

GPS Coordinates for Words

Think of each word as a city on a map, placed by GPS coordinates. Cat, Dog, and Tiger are all in the same "Animal District" — their coordinates are close. Airplane is in a completely different region: "Vehicles."

The distance between coordinates tells you how related words are — unlike one-hot encoding where everything is equally far apart.

CBOW — Continuous Bag of Words

⚙ Sentence
The Sun rises ← TARGET in east

Blue = context words (input). Green = target word the model must predict.

⚙ Averaging Calculation
The = [0.2, 0.3]
Sun = [0.8, 0.9]
in = [0.1, 0.4]
east = [0.5, 0.7]

Average = sum / 4:
[0.2+0.8+0.1+0.5] / 4 = 0.400
[0.3+0.9+0.4+0.7] / 4 = 0.575

Context vector = [0.400, 0.575]

Prediction Probabilities:

rises
0.91 ✓
sets
0.05
shines
0.04
💡 Analogy

Fill-in-the-Blank Exam

On an exam you see: "The Sun ___ in the east."

You look at all the surrounding words — Sun, east, The — and use them together to figure out the missing word.

Many clues in → one answer out. The more context available, the better the prediction. CBOW is fast and works well on frequent words.

Skip-Gram

⚙ Technical — Reverse of CBOW

Given one center word, predict all surrounding context words.

Input: rises

Predicts →

The Sun in east
One word in → many context words out. Better for rare words than CBOW.
📊 Animated Skip-Gram
💡 Analogy

Celebrity Predicts Their Crowd

Put Messi in the center of a room. Who would you expect to find around him? Football, Argentina, Goal, Barcelona, Trophy, Champions League…

Skip-gram works like a celebrity who predicts their own social surroundings — given one famous word, it predicts the entire circle of words that tend to appear nearby.

6

Negative Sampling

⚙ Technical

Instead of updating all 50,000+ vocabulary words per training step, negative sampling compares the target against a small random sample of "wrong" words.

Word Pairs:

✅ Cat ↔ Milk (positive)
❌ Cat ↔ Airplane (negative)
Task becomes binary classification: "Are these two words neighbours? Yes/No?" — instead of predicting over the entire vocabulary.
💡 Analogy

Dating App Shortcut

Instead of meeting every single person on Earth to find a good match, a dating app shows you just a handful of candidates.

You compare your profile against a small random sample — not the entire global population. That's exactly what negative sampling does.

It makes training dramatically faster with barely any quality loss.

Cosine Similarity

⚙ Formula
cos(θ) = (A · B) / (‖A‖ × ‖B‖)
dot product ÷ product of magnitudes
Cat
0.80.7
Dog
0.750.72
Cosine(Cat, Dog) ≈ 0.98 — very similar!
Range: −1 (opposite) → 0 (unrelated) → 1 (same direction)
📊 Vector Angle Visualization
💡 Analogy

Direction Matters More Than Distance

Two cars could be 100km apart but heading in the exact same direction — they're on the same road trip. Two other cars might be parked side-by-side but heading in opposite directions — completely different journeys.

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two arrows, not their length. Small angle = similar meaning, regardless of word frequency.

GloVe — Global Vectors

⚙ Technical

GloVe builds a global co-occurrence matrix from the entire corpus, then learns embeddings that capture both local and global word statistics.

Word2Vec: local sliding window. GloVe: entire corpus statistics.

💡 Analogy

Studying the Entire School's Social Network

Word2Vec is like eavesdropping on a few individual conversations.

GloVe is like studying the entire school's yearbook — who sat with whom at lunch, who was in the same clubs, who showed up in the same photos, across every event all year.

By looking at the global pattern of who appears near whom, GloVe captures richer, more stable relationships.

King − Man + Woman = Queen

⚙ Vector Arithmetic

Embeddings capture semantic relationships so precisely that you can do arithmetic with meaning:

King
−
Man
+
Woman
≈
Queen 👑
The direction Man→Woman encodes the concept of gender. Adding that direction to King lands you at Queen. The model learned gender as a geometric direction in space.
💡 Analogy

Adding and Removing Characteristics

Start with King: royal ruler, leads a kingdom, wears a crown, is male.

Remove "male" characteristics (− Man).

Add "female" characteristics (+ Woman).

What do you get? A royal female ruler — a Queen. Embeddings don't just store words — they store the mathematical structure of meaning itself.

10

Porter Stemmer

⚙ Technical

Reduces words to their root stem so different grammatical forms are treated as the same token.

OriginalStem
playing→play
played→play
plays→play
studying→studi
studies→studi
from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer

ps = PorterStemmer()
words = ["playing", "played", "plays"]
for w in words:
    print(ps.stem(w)) # → play
💡 Analogy

The Library Shelf Rule

A smart librarian places "run," "running," "runs," and "runner" all on the same shelf under "run" — because they're all about the same core concept.

Porter Stemmer is that librarian. It doesn't care about grammatical form — it finds the core idea and groups everything together.

This reduces vocabulary size and improves matching accuracy in summarization and search tasks.

💡 Analogy

The Newspaper Editor

A seasoned editor receives a 2,000-word article and needs to cut it to 200 words for the front page.

They don't pick randomly — they identify which sentences contain the most important keywords, score each sentence by importance, then select the top ones.

That's our pipeline: clean the text → count key words → score sentences → extract the best ones.

Stopwords removed: "the," "is," "a," "in," "of" — these carry no unique information and inflate word counts.

Real Industry Example — Merchant Matching

⚙ Problem — Same Company, Different Names

In financial data, the same company appears under many different names. Without embeddings, they look completely different to a computer.

NETFLIX
NETFLIX.COM
NETFLIX INC
→
✅ Same Entity
Character-level embeddings + cosine similarity allow the model to recognize these as the same merchant — critical for fraud detection, reconciliation, and analytics.
💡 Analogy

Robert, Rob, Bobby — Same Person

You meet someone named Robert. His friends call him Rob. His grandmother calls him Bobby. His LinkedIn says Bob Smith.

A smart human knows these are all the same person. A naive computer sees four completely different strings and treats them as strangers.

Embeddings teach the computer that surface-level variation ≠ different meaning.

NLP Representation Evolution — Same Sentence, Different Models

⚙ The Test Sentence
Two sentences that mean the opposite — we'll track how each model handles them
Sentence A: "The dog bit the man."
Sentence B: "The man bit the dog."

Same words. Same vocabulary. Completely opposite meaning. Watch how each model handles — or fails to handle — word order.

📦 Bag of Words — circa 1990s

Each sentence becomes a word frequency count. Words are thrown into a bag — order is completely discarded.

❌ Sentence A representation
the: 2   dog: 1   bit: 1   man: 1
❌ Sentence B representation
the: 2   man: 1   bit: 1   dog: 1
🚫 Fatal flaw: Both sentences produce the exact same representation. The model has zero ability to tell "dog bit man" from "man bit dog." Word order is completely invisible.
What it can do: Topic detection ("this sentence is about dogs and biting"), keyword search, simple spam filtering.

Also known as TF-IDF when weighted by rarity. Still no word order.

🔵 Word2Vec — 2013

Each word gets its own dense vector, learned from co-occurrence patterns. To represent a sentence, Word2Vec typically averages all word vectors.

the
[0.12, 0.04]
dog
[0.81, 0.73]
bit
[0.54, 0.62]
the
[0.12, 0.04]
man
[0.78, 0.69]
Sentence A avg: ([0.12+0.81+0.54+0.12+0.78]/5, ...) = [0.474, 0.424]
Sentence B avg: ([0.12+0.78+0.54+0.12+0.81]/5, ...) = [0.474, 0.424]
❌ Sentence A vector
[0.474, 0.424, ...]
"The dog bit the man"
❌ Sentence B vector
[0.474, 0.424, ...]
"The man bit the dog"
🚫 Same problem as BoW at sentence level: Averaging is commutative — adding vectors in any order gives the same result. A+B+C = C+B+A. The model still cannot distinguish who bit whom.
✅ Major upgrade over BoW: Individual word vectors now carry meaning. "dog" and "wolf" are close. "dog" and "keyboard" are far. Semantic similarity works beautifully at the word level.
💡 Key insight: Word2Vec trains on a sliding window of ~5 words — it captures local context around each word, but when you collapse a sentence to a single vector, order disappears.
🟣 GloVe — 2014

GloVe learns from a global co-occurrence matrix — how often word pairs appear together across the entire corpus. Same averaging problem applies at sentence level.

GloVe co-occurrence counts (from large corpus):

dog ↔ bite: 4,821
man ↔ bite: 1,203
dog ↔ man: 6,102

GloVe vectors for "dog" are shaped by ALL contexts where dog appeared — across millions of sentences. Richer than Word2Vec's local window.

❌ Still the same after averaging
Sentence A → avg([the, dog, bit, the, man])
Sentence B → avg([the, man, bit, the, dog])
Same result.
✅ Richer individual word vectors
"dog" vector better captures: pet, animal, loyal, bark, fetch — because it's trained on global stats, not just a local window.
🚫 Order still not captured at sentence level: GloVe word vectors are static — "bank" has the same vector whether it means river bank or financial bank. And averaging sentences still discards order.
✅ GloVe advantage over Word2Vec: Word vectors are more globally consistent. Word2Vec might miss relationships that only emerge across the full corpus. GloVe sees everything at once.
🟡 FastText — 2016

FastText's key innovation: it breaks words into character n-grams (subword units). This solves out-of-vocabulary words and captures morphology — but still averages for sentences.

biting
→
<bi
bit
iti
tin
ing
ng>

Each subword chunk gets its own vector. The word vector = sum of its subword vectors. Powerful for morphologically rich languages.

✅ New word "bitten" → still works
bitten = <bi + bit + itt + tte + ten + en>
Shares subwords with "bite", "bitter" — gets a reasonable vector even if never seen in training.
❌ Order: still averaging
Sentence A and B still collapse to the same averaged vector. "Dog bit man" = "Man bit dog."
✅ FastText wins on: Rare words, misspellings, morphology ("running" shares subwords with "run"), multilingual text, out-of-vocabulary words.
🚫 Still no word order at sentence level. All three — Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText — are static embeddings. Every word has one fixed vector regardless of context.
💡 The fundamental ceiling of static embeddings: "I went to the river bank" and "I went to the bank for a loan" — "bank" gets the same vector in Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText. Context-blindness.
🟢 BERT — 2018

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) is the first major model to produce contextual embeddings. Every word's vector changes depending on its neighbors — in both directions.

[CLS]
The
dog
bit
the
man
[SEP]

BERT adds special tokens [CLS] (classification) and [SEP] (separator). Crucially it also adds positional encodings — each position gets a unique signal baked into the input.

Attention heatmap — "dog" attending to other words in Sentence A:

Darker = stronger attention. "dog" pays most attention to "bit" and "the man" — it knows it's the subject who performed the action.

✅ Sentence A — "The dog bit the man"
Vector for "dog": [0.82, 0.71, −0.34, ...]
"dog" = subject / actor
✅ Sentence B — "The man bit the dog"
Vector for "dog": [0.14, 0.88, 0.67, ...]
"dog" = object / receiver
✅ BERT breakthrough: Same word, different positions → different vectors. "dog" as subject vs. object is now mathematically distinguishable. Word order is fully captured via positional encoding + self-attention.
🚫 BERT limitation: It's an encoder — great at understanding and classification, but not designed for text generation. It sees the full sentence bidirectionally but can't generate new text token by token.
⚫ GPT — 2018 → ChatGPT 2022

GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) uses a causal decoder — it processes tokens left-to-right, and each token can only attend to previous tokens. This makes it ideal for generation.

The
dog
bit
the
man
.
// Causal (left-to-right) attention mask:
The → can attend to: [The]
dog → can attend to: [The, dog]
bit → can attend to: [The, dog, bit]
the → can attend to: [The, dog, bit, the]
man → can attend to: [The, dog, bit, the, man]
→ Next token prediction at each step
✅ Sentence A — GPT processing
After "The dog bit" → GPT assigns high probability to "the man" as next tokens. It knows dog is agent, man is patient — because position and left-context are fully tracked.
✅ Sentence B — GPT processing
After "The man bit" → GPT now assigns high probability to "the dog." Completely different internal state. Same words, different order → different model behavior.
✅ GPT solves everything above: Positional encodings + causal attention means every token's representation is shaped by its exact position and all prior context. Word order is a first-class citizen.
✅ Scale changes everything: GPT-3 (175B parameters) and GPT-4 develop emergent understanding — reasoning, analogies, multi-step problem solving — that no static embedding could approach.
💡 BERT vs GPT: BERT = reads the whole sentence at once (bidirectional) → great for understanding. GPT = reads left to right → great for generation. ChatGPT uses a GPT-style decoder fine-tuned with human feedback (RLHF).
