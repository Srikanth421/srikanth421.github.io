Why Were Transformers Needed? The problem with what you already know

⚙ The Core Problem

Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText produce static word embeddings. Each word gets one fixed vector — but to represent a whole sentence, you average all vectors together.

Averaging is commutative:

A + B + C  = same result as
C + B + A

So these two sentences produce identical vectors:

dogbitesman→ avg = [0.55, 0.61]
manbitesdog→ avg = [0.55, 0.61]
🚫 The model cannot tell who bit whom. Word order is invisible.

Also: Word2Vec gives "bank" the same vector in "river bank" and "bank loan". Context is ignored completely.

💡 Analogy

A Bag of Scrabble Tiles

Imagine tipping the words of a sentence into a bag and shaking it up. You lose all sense of order.

"The chef cooked the fish" and "The fish cooked the chef" become the same bag — same tiles, different meaning, different meal.

Word2Vec is that bag. Rich word meaning, zero sentence structure.

💡 This is the gap Transformers were designed to fill: meaning + order + context, all at once.

RNNs & CNNs — What Came Before Transformers

⚙ Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs / LSTMs)

RNNs process words one at a time, left to right. Each step's output feeds into the next step — creating a chain of hidden states.

The
step 1
→
cat
step 2
→
sat
step 3
→
on
step 4
→ ⋯

Two critical problems:

🚫 Vanishing gradient: By word 50, the memory of word 1 has almost completely faded. Long-range dependencies are lost — the network "forgets" early words.
🚫 No parallelization: Step 5 must wait for step 4, which waits for step 3… Training is inherently sequential and slow. Cannot leverage GPUs efficiently.

LSTMs (Long Short-Term Memory) partially fixed the memory problem with "gates" that control what to remember/forget — but they're still sequential and still struggle beyond ~200 words.

💡 Analogy — The Conveyor Belt

RNN = Reading Through a Keyhole

Imagine reading a book but you can only see one word at a time through a tiny keyhole. You slide the book left, word by word. You try to remember what you read earlier, but by page 10, you've forgotten the details of page 1.

Word 1: "The" — 💡 clear memory
Word 10: "because" — 🔅 fading memory of "The"
Word 50: "tired" — 💤 word 1 is practically gone

LSTMs added a "notebook" to jot down important things — but the notebook has limited space and still fills up on long documents.

⚙ Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs for Text)

CNNs slide a fixed-size window (e.g., 3–5 words) across the sentence, detecting local patterns like word combinations and phrases.

The
cat
sat
on
the
wall
↑ window size = 3, slides right →
🚫 Limited range: A window of 3 words can't directly connect word 1 to word 80. You'd need to stack many layers, and even then long-range connections are weak.

CNNs are great for images (detecting edges → shapes → objects). For text, they capture local phrases ("not good", "very happy") but miss sentence-level relationships.

💡 Analogy — The Magnifying Glass

CNN = Reading With a Magnifying Glass

You hold a magnifying glass over a newspaper. You can see 3–5 words at a time in sharp detail — great for spotting specific phrases.

But you can never see the whole article at once. If the first sentence says "John" and the last sentence says "he", you can't connect them without moving the glass back and forth many times.

The one-liner: RNNs read one word at a time and forget early words. CNNs look through a small window and miss distant relationships. Transformers let every word see every other word simultaneously — that's why they won.

Transformer Overview The big picture

⚙ What Is a Transformer?

A Transformer is a neural network architecture built entirely on attention mechanisms — no recurrence, no convolutions.

It has two main parts:

  • Encoder — reads and understands the input
  • Decoder — generates the output
📥 Input Sentence
↓
🔡 Word Embeddings + Position
↓
🧠 Encoder (understand)
↓
💡 Context Vector
↓
✍ Decoder (generate)
↓
📤 Output Sentence
💡 Analogy

The Human Translator

Imagine a professional translator working on a Spanish document:

  1. Read the full English sentence, holding the entire meaning in mind
  2. Understand not just words, but tone, context, and relationships
  3. Generate the Spanish translation one word at a time, referencing understanding
Input (English):
"The cat sat on the wall"
Output (Spanish):
"El gato se sentó en la pared"
Key insight: the translator doesn't just look up each word. They understand the whole sentence first — that's what the Encoder does.

Positional Encoding Teaching the model word order

⚙ Technical

Attention mechanisms process all words simultaneously — they have no built-in sense of order. Positional Encoding fixes this by adding a unique position signal to each word's embedding.

PE(pos, 2i)  = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d))
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d))

// d = embedding dimension, pos = word position

The final input to the transformer is:

Input = Word Embedding + Positional Encoding

Now "dog" at position 1 has a different combined vector than "dog" at position 3. Order is preserved.

In self-attention, each word computes a weighted relationship with every other word in the sentence — including itself. The output is a context-aware representation.

💡 Analogy

The Detective Solving a Mystery

A great detective doesn't hear one clue in isolation. When they encounter the word "it", they immediately scan every previous clue in the case file looking for what "it" refers to.

🔍 Detective's Notebook:
Clue 1 — "The cat"  →  Strong lead ★★★
Clue 2 — "the wall"  →  Possible ★★
Clue 3 — "sat, on, the"  →  Weak ★
Conclusion: "it" = the cat (70% confidence)

Self-attention is that detective. For every word, it scores how relevant every other word is — then uses those scores to build a richer, context-aware understanding.

💡 Key insight: Word2Vec gives "it" a single fixed vector. Self-attention gives "it" a different vector in every sentence, depending on context.

Attention Calculation Worked example — what does "it" refer to?

⚙ Full Step-by-Step — "The cat sat on the wall because it was tired"

Let's trace exactly how the model figures out that "it" = "cat", using the Q·K·V mechanism from Section 4.

Step 0 — Generate Q, K, V vectors

Each word is multiplied by three learned weight matrices (WQ, WK, WV) to produce its Query, Key, and Value vectors:

// Each word × learned weight matrix → vector

"it" × W_Q → Query("it") = [2.1, 0.8, 1.5, 0.3] ← "what do I refer to?"
"cat" × W_K → Key("cat") = [1.9, 0.9, 1.4, 0.5] ← "I'm an animal noun"
"on" × W_K → Key("on") = [0.1, 0.2, 0.8, 2.1] ← "I'm a preposition"

"cat" × W_V → Value("cat") = [0.7, 0.9, 0.2, 0.8] ← actual cat information
"on" × W_V → Value("on") = [0.1, 0.3, 0.1, 0.1] ← actual "on" information
💡 Notice: Query("it") and Key("cat") have similar values — they point in the same direction. Key("on") points elsewhere. The model learned this alignment during training by seeing millions of sentences where pronouns refer to nouns.
Step 1 — Query × Key → Raw Score
// Dot product: how similar is "it" to each word?

Q("it") · K("cat")
= (2.1×1.9)+(0.8×0.9)+(1.5×1.4)+(0.3×0.5)
= 3.99 + 0.72 + 2.10 + 0.15
= 6.96 ← high! similar direction

Q("it") · K("on")
= (2.1×0.1)+(0.8×0.2)+(1.5×0.8)+(0.3×2.1)
= 0.21 + 0.16 + 1.20 + 0.63
= 2.20 ← low, different direction
Step 2 — ÷ √d (scale)
d_k = 4 dimensions
√d_k = √4 = 2

cat: 6.96 / 2 = 3.48
on: 2.20 / 2 = 1.10

// Why scale? Large dot products push
// softmax into regions where gradients
// vanish. Dividing keeps values healthy.
Step 3 — Softmax → Attention Weights
e^3.48 = 32.46
e^1.10 = 3.00
Total = 35.46

cat: 32.46 / 35.46 = 0.92 ← 92% attention!
on: 3.00 / 35.46 = 0.08 ← 8% attention
                    ────
                    1.00 ✓

Softmax amplifies the gap — 3.48 vs 1.10 becomes 0.92 vs 0.08. The winner takes almost everything.

Step 4 — Weights × Values
0.92 × Value("cat") = 0.92 × [0.7, 0.9, 0.2, 0.8]
                  = [0.644, 0.828, 0.184, 0.736]

0.08 × Value("on") = 0.08 × [0.1, 0.3, 0.1, 0.1]
                  = [0.008, 0.024, 0.008, 0.008]
Step 5 — Sum → Context Vector Output
Output("it") = [0.644, 0.828, 0.184, 0.736]
             + [0.008, 0.024, 0.008, 0.008]
             = [0.652, 0.852, 0.192, 0.744]

// This is 92% cat information + 8% "on"
// The vector for "it" now carries the
// meaning of "cat" — pronoun resolved! ✅
📊 The punchline: The original embedding of "it" was generic and context-free. After self-attention, the output vector for "it" is 92% cat — because the Query-Key match told the model what to pay attention to, and the Value told it what information to absorb.

Multi-Head Attention Many perspectives at once

⚙ Technical

Instead of one attention computation, run multiple attention heads in parallel, each learning different relationships.

h = 8 heads    // typical in base Transformer
d = 512 total dimensions
d_head = 512 / 8 = 64 per head

Output = Concat(head₁, …, head₈) × W°
= [64×8] = 512 dimensions

Each head independently learns to attend to different patterns:

🔵 Head 1
Subject-Verb
🟢 Head 2
Pronouns
🟠 Head 3
Sentiment
🟣 Head 4
Topic
🔴 Head 5
Negation
🩵 Head 6
Tense
🪻 Head 7
Entities
🟡 Head 8
Coreference
All 8 outputs are concatenated → projected → one rich representation
💡 Analogy

The Crime Investigation Team

One detective might miss something. A great police department sends a whole team, each specializing in a different angle:

🔵
Grammar Detective — finds who is the subject
🟢
Pronoun Detective — resolves "it", "he", "they"
🟠
Sentiment Detective — positive, negative, neutral?
🟣
Topic Detective — is this about sports? Finance?

After each detective files their report, the chief combines all findings into one comprehensive understanding. That's concatenation + projection.

💡 One attention head would make one guess. Eight heads together build a 360° understanding of every sentence.
⚙ Worked Example — Continuing "The cat sat on the wall because it was tired"

In Section 5, we used d=4 dimensions. Now let's split those into 2 heads (2 dimensions each) and see how each head finds something different about "it".

Step 1 — Split vectors into heads
// Original Q, K, V have d=4 dimensions
// Split into h=2 heads → d_head = 4/2 = 2 dims each

Query("it") = [2.1, 0.8, 1.5, 0.3]
    Head 1: [2.1, 0.8]   Head 2: [1.5, 0.3]

Key("cat") = [1.9, 0.9, 1.4, 0.5]
    Head 1: [1.9, 0.9]   Head 2: [1.4, 0.5]

Key("wall") = [0.4, 0.7, 1.6, 0.9]
    Head 1: [0.4, 0.7]   Head 2: [1.6, 0.9]
🔵 Head 1 — Pronoun Detective
// Q("it") · K for each word (dims 0-1 only)

[2.1, 0.8] · [1.9, 0.9] = 3.99+0.72 = 4.71 cat
[2.1, 0.8] · [0.4, 0.7] = 0.84+0.56 = 1.40 wall

Softmax → cat: 0.96  wall: 0.04

// Head 1 says: "it" = cat (96%)
Output₁ = [0.68, 0.87]
Head 1 learned to resolve pronouns. In these 2 dimensions, "it" strongly matches "cat."
🟢 Head 2 — Location Detective
// Q("it") · K for each word (dims 2-3 only)

[1.5, 0.3] · [1.4, 0.5] = 2.10+0.15 = 2.25 cat
[1.5, 0.3] · [1.6, 0.9] = 2.40+0.27 = 2.67 wall

Softmax → cat: 0.40  wall: 0.60

// Head 2 says: "it" is near wall (60%)
Output₂ = [0.52, 0.41]
Head 2 learned spatial context. In these 2 dimensions, "it" relates more to "wall" — where the cat is located.
Step 2 — Concatenate + Project
// Glue outputs back together
Concat = Head1 ⊕ Head2
       = [0.68, 0.87] ⊕ [0.52, 0.41]
       = [0.68, 0.87, 0.52, 0.41] ← back to d=4

// Multiply by output projection matrix W_O
Final = Concat × W_O = [0.71, 0.83, 0.48, 0.55]

// This single vector now encodes:
// • "it" refers to "cat" (from Head 1)
// • "it" is located at the "wall" (from Head 2)
// • Both perspectives fused into one representation ✅
Why this matters: A single attention head in Section 5 only discovered "it = cat." With two heads, the model simultaneously learned that "it" refers to the cat AND that the cat is on the wall. In real Transformers with 8–16 heads, the model captures grammar, sentiment, coreference, topic, negation, and more — all in one pass.

Encoder Block How understanding is built

⚙ Architecture

One encoder block repeats N times (N=6 in original Transformer). Each pass deepens understanding:

Encoder Block (×6)
📥 Input Embeddings + Position
↓
🧠 Multi-Head Self-Attention
↓
➕ Add & Norm (Residual)
↓
⚡ Feed-Forward Network
↓
➕ Add & Norm (Residual)
↓
📤 Enriched Representation

Key components explained:

Residual Connection (Add): Output = Layer(x) + x. Passes original input alongside transformed version — prevents gradient vanishing.
Layer Norm: Normalises values to have mean≈0, std≈1. Keeps training stable.
Feed-Forward Network: Two linear layers with ReLU. Adds non-linearity — the model can learn complex patterns.
💡 Analogy

Reading a Novel Six Times

Imagine reading a complex novel once. You get the gist. Read it again — you notice foreshadowing. Read it a third time — themes emerge.

Each encoder pass is like another careful reading of the sentence. Relationships get richer, patterns get clearer, understanding gets deeper.

Residual connection = you never forget what you read before. Each pass adds to — not replaces — your understanding.

Pass 1: basic word meanings
Pass 2: sentence structure
Pass 3: pronoun resolution
Pass 4: tone and sentiment
Pass 5: topic and domain
Pass 6: nuance and implication
✅ After 6 passes, "it was tired" is fully resolved to "the cat was tired" — the model has built a complete understanding.

FFN(x) = ReLU(x · W₁ + b₁) · W₂ + b₂
Applied independently to each word. Transforms each vector through non-linear layers. Think of it as: attention figured out what's related, FFN now processes what that relationship means.

Decoder Block Generating the output

⚙ Architecture

The decoder has three sub-layers instead of two, and generates output one token at a time:

Decoder Block (×6)
🔒 Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention
↓
➕ Add & Norm
↓
🔗 Cross-Attention (Encoder ↔ Decoder)
↓
➕ Add & Norm
↓
⚡ Feed-Forward Network
↓
➕ Add & Norm
↓
🎯 Linear + Softmax → Next Word
Cross-Attention: The decoder's Query comes from the decoder, but Keys and Values come from the encoder output. This is how the decoder "reads" the encoder's understanding.
💡 Analogy

Writing an Essay One Word at a Time

Imagine you've just read a detailed brief (encoder's job). Now you must write your response — but with one strict rule: you can only look at what you've already written, never at future words.

Step 1: [START] → writes "El"
Step 2: El → writes "gato"
Step 3: El gato → writes "se"
Step 4: El gato se → writes "sentó"
Step 5: El gato se sentó → writes "en"
…

At every step the decoder:

  1. Looks at its own words so far (masked self-attention)
  2. Looks at the encoder's full understanding (cross-attention)
  3. Predicts the next best word

Masking Preventing the decoder from cheating

⚙ Technical

During training, the decoder sees the full target sentence — but it must not use future tokens to predict the current token. Masking enforces this.

Setting future scores to −∞ before softmax means e^(−∞) = 0 — those positions get exactly zero attention weight.
💡 Analogy

The Covered Answer Sheet

Imagine sitting an exam where the questions are printed below your answer box. The invigilator places a card over all the questions you haven't reached yet.

When answering question 3, you can:

  • ✅ See questions 1 and 2 (and your own answers)
  • ❌ NOT see questions 4, 5, 6… (covered!)
Predicting "love" → can see: "I" ✓
Predicting "football" → can see: "I love" ✓
Cannot peek at "football" while predicting "love" ✗
💡 Why mask? So the model genuinely learns to predict — if it could see the answer, it would just copy rather than learn patterns.

At inference time (real use), masking is natural — future words don't exist yet anyway!

Concept What It Does Analogy
Positional
Encoding		 Adds a unique position signal to each word's vector so the model knows word order (sin/cos functions) 🎭 Theatre seat numbers — same actors, but seat 1 ≠ seat 10
Self-Attention Each word computes a weighted relationship with every other word. Produces context-aware representations 🔍 Detective scanning all clues to resolve what "it" refers to
Query (Q) A vector representing what the current word is searching for in the sentence ❓ "I want spicy food" — your search query
Key (K) A vector representing what information each word contains / offers 🏷 Restaurant name tag — "Thai Curry: spicy, hot"
Value (V) The actual information each word contributes to the output, weighted by attention scores 📦 The actual dish served after you chose Thai Curry
Softmax Converts raw attention scores into probabilities that sum to 1. Amplifies the biggest scores 📊 Voting system — spreads 100 votes across candidates proportionally
Multi-Head
Attention		 Runs N parallel attention computations, each learning different relationships. Concatenates results 🕵 Crime team — grammar, pronoun, sentiment, topic detectives working simultaneously
Residual
Connection		 Output = Layer(x) + x. Preserves original information alongside the transformed version 📚 Re-reading a book but never forgetting the previous reads
Layer Norm Normalises the output to have zero mean and unit variance. Keeps training stable ⚖ Adjusting the volume between songs on a playlist to a consistent level
Encoder Reads the full input bidirectionally, building a rich contextual representation of meaning 🧠 The reader — absorbs and understands the source text completely
Decoder Generates output tokens one at a time, attending to encoder output and its own previous outputs ✍ The writer — produces the response word by word, referencing their notes
